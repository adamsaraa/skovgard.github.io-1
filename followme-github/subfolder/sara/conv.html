If you don’t look foreign enough, you may suddenly find yourself being asked a question in fast, mumbled Danish by the cashier or another friendly shopper in the local supermarket.

Learn some Danish and pretend you’ve been living here for your whole life.

Here is a little Danish supermarket vocabulary you should learn to use. It’s simple, it’s a great way to boost confidence while trying to speak the language.

The best part is that you have the same conversation 99,9% of the time, so it gets easier to understand what is being said.

via GIPHY

Medarbejder (employee): Hej! (Hi!)
Dig (you): Hej! (Hi!)
M: 93 kr. På beløbet? (On the amount?)
D: Ja, tak. (Yes, thank you.)
M: Ellers andet? (Something else?)
D: Ja... / Nej tak. / Ellers tak. (Yes.../ No, thank you. / No, thank you.)
M: Kvittering med? (Receipt with?)
D: Undskyld hvad sagde du? (Sorry, what did you say?)
M: Kvittering? Bon? (Receipt? Ticket?)
D: Ja... / Nej tak. / Ellers tak. (Yes.../ No, thank you. / No, thank you.)
M: Skal du have en pose med? (Do you want to buy a bag?)
D: Ellers tak, jeg har min egen. (No, thank you. I have my own.) En stor/en lille pose tak. (A large/small one, thank you.)
M: God aften / God dag / God weekend / Hej, hej (Good evening/Good day/Good weekend/ Bye-bye)
D: Tak, i lige måde. (Thank you, same to you!)

'På beløbet'??

On the amount? Confusing, isn’t it? Because it is common in Danish supermarkets and some Danish corner shops to withdraw cash by paying an extra amount with your bill and then get it in cash, you will often find cashiers asking this.

via GIPHY

For example, you buy groceries for 80 dkk and then you ask the cashier if you can have 100 dkk in cash.You pay 180 dkk on your card and get back 100 dkk in cash.

Dig: Kan jeg få 100 kroner over? (Can I get 100 kroner extra?)
Medarbejder: Ja, selvfølgelig. Værsgo. (Yes, of course. There you go.)
